# ADR-0013: RFC/ADR Minimum Content Requirement (Mandatory Alternatives Section)

**Status:** Final
**Date:** 2026-07-13
**Supersedes:** —
**Related RFCs:** RFC-0031
**Constitution Amendment:** Extends Definition of Approved (Principle 1, §Quality Gates)

---

## Context

Constitution Principle 8 (Document Decisions) states that every significant architectural choice must be recorded. However, the existing RFC and ADR corpus has evolved a de facto format that is dangerously minimal: the average ADR is 15-20 lines with only Context, Decision, and Consections sections, and no Alternatives Considered section. Only RFC-0001 includes an alternatives analysis, and even that is brief.

This minimal format works when the corpus is small (10 ADRs, ~15 RFCs) and the original authors are available to answer questions. But the agentx project is designed as a 10-year platform. By document #50, the original context for these decisions will be lost, and the documents will fail their intended purpose as institutional memory. A future architect reading ADR-0001 cannot understand WHY at-least-once delivery was chosen over exactly-once — only THAT it was chosen. This makes the ADR corpus a record of conclusions without reasoning, which is nearly useless for future decision-making.

This problem was identified in the Architecture Assessment (score 3.0/5.0) and in RFC-0031 (Governance Process Improvements), which proposed mandatory minimum content requirements. This ADR implements RFC-0031's proposal and, by extension, modifies the Constitution's own "Definition of Approved" quality gate — a decision that must itself be recorded as an ADR per Principle 1.

Critically, this ADR is self-applying: it establishes a standard that it itself must meet. This ADR includes the Alternatives Considered section it mandates for all future documents.

## Decision

Every RFC and ADR in the agentx corpus MUST meet the following minimum content requirements, effective immediately:

### RFC Requirements
1. **Problem Statement:** A clear description of the problem or question the RFC addresses.
2. **Proposed Solution:** A detailed description of the proposed approach.
3. **Alternatives Considered:** At least 2 alternatives, each with:
   - A brief description of the alternative approach.
   - A clear reason for rejection (or, if the alternative is chosen, why it is preferred).
4. **Open Questions:** Any unresolved questions that may affect the decision.

### ADR Requirements
1. **Context:** 2-3 paragraphs explaining the situation and why this decision is needed NOW.
2. **Decision:** A clear, unambiguous statement of the decision.
3. **Alternatives Considered:** At least 2 alternatives (including the chosen one), each with:
   - A description of the alternative.
   - Pros and cons.
   - For rejected alternatives: a clear explanation of why they were not chosen.
4. **Consequences:** Bulleted list covering positive impacts, negative impacts, impact on existing contracts, and when to revisit.

### Enforcement Mechanism
A CI lint check validates the presence of required section headers in all RFC and ADR markdown files. The check uses a simple regex-based section header scanner:
- For RFCs: `## Problem Statement`, `## Proposed Solution`, `## Alternatives Considered` must all be present.
- For ADRs: `## Context`, `## Decision`, `## Alternatives Considered`, `## Consequences` must all be present.

Documents missing required sections fail the "Specification Review" quality gate defined in the Engineering Execution Program §8. This means they cannot progress from Draft to Specification Review, and a Volume whose RFC fails this check cannot be marked Approved.

### Grandfathering
Existing RFCs and ADRs (ADR-0001 through ADR-0010, RFC-0001 through RFC-0030) are NOT retroactively required to meet this standard. They retain their current format. However, if an existing ADR is superseded by a new ADR, the new ADR must follow this format and may optionally include a summary of the original decision's reasoning.

### Self-Application
This ADR (ADR-0013) is the first document to be written under this standard. Its Alternatives Considered section below demonstrates the expected level of analysis.

## Alternatives Considered

### Option A: Keep Current Short Format
**Description:** Maintain the existing de facto format (Context, Decision, Consequences, ~15-20 lines). No enforcement mechanism.
**Pros:**
- Lowest authoring friction. Authors can record decisions quickly.
- No CI infrastructure changes needed.
- Avoids the risk of authors "gaming" the format by writing padding to meet requirements.
- Works adequately for a small corpus where the original authors are available.
**Cons:**
- By document #50, the corpus will function as a list of conclusions without reasoning. Future architects cannot understand WHY decisions were made.
- Silent violations accumulate because the short format makes it easy to omit critical reasoning (e.g., security tradeoffs, performance considerations).
- The corpus fails Constitution Principle 8's intent ("Document Decisions") even if it complies with the letter.
- External reviewers (security auditors, enterprise customers) will find the decision record insufficient for due diligence.
- The Assessment already flagged this as a deficiency contributing to the 3.0/5.0 score.
**Why not chosen:** The primary purpose of ADRs and RFCs is institutional memory, not speed of authorship. A decision record that omits the reasoning behind the decision is not a decision record — it's a changelog. The short format is appropriate for a weekend prototype, not a 10-year platform with a 14-Volume architecture handbook.

### Option B: Mandatory 500-Word Minimum
**Description:** Require every RFC and ADR to contain at least 500 words of content. No specific section requirements — only a word count threshold enforced by CI.
**Pros:**
- Simple to implement and enforce (word count is trivially checkable).
- Guarantees a minimum level of detail in all documents.
- Easy to communicate to authors ("write at least 500 words").
**Cons:**
- Encourages padding. Authors will add filler text to meet the word count, degrading signal-to-noise ratio.
- Does not ensure the RIGHT content is present. A 500-word RFC with no alternatives section is worse than a 200-word RFC with a clear alternatives analysis.
- Word count is a proxy for quality that is easily gamed.
- Makes simple decisions (e.g., "we use TypeScript, not JavaScript") artificially burdensome to document.
- Does not address the specific gap identified: the absence of alternatives analysis.
**Why not chosen:** Word count targets are a well-known anti-pattern in documentation governance. They optimize for the wrong metric (length) and create perverse incentives (padding). The actual problem is missing structural elements (alternatives, consequences), not insufficient length. Section-header requirements directly address the structural gap.

### Option C: Required Section Headers with Substantive Content (Chosen)
**Description:** Define mandatory section headers for RFCs and ADRs. CI validates header presence. A qualitative review step (part of Specification Review) validates that sections contain substantive content, not placeholder text.
**Pros:**
- Directly addresses the identified gap: missing alternatives analysis.
- Enforces the right structure without prescribing length — a simple decision gets a simple (but complete) ADR; a complex decision gets a detailed one.
- CI enforcement ensures no document slips through without the required sections.
- The qualitative review step catches low-quality content (padding, copy-paste) without a mechanistic word count.
- Self-reinforcing: as the corpus grows with well-structured documents, authors have better examples to follow.
- Consistent with RFC-0031's proposal, ensuring governance traceability.
**Cons:**
- Adds authoring friction for decisions that genuinely have no meaningful alternatives (e.g., "we use Node.js because the team knows it"). Authors must still document at least 2 alternatives, even if the rejection is obvious.
- The qualitative review step introduces subjectivity — different reviewers may disagree on whether content is "substantive."
- CI check is a necessary but not sufficient condition. A document could have all headers with one-sentence content under each. The Specification Review quality gate is the real enforcement, but it depends on human attention.
- Increases the time from RFC Draft to Specification Review by an estimated 30-60 minutes per document.
- Risk of process bloat if the section requirements grow over time ("section creep").

## Consequences

- **Positive:** Every future decision in the corpus will have a recorded rationale, fulfilling Constitution Principle 8's intent.
- **Positive:** The CI lint check provides a hard floor that no document can fall below, preventing regression to the current minimal format.
- **Positive:** Future architects, security auditors, and enterprise customers can trace the reasoning behind every architectural decision.
- **Positive:** RFC-0031 is fully implemented, closing a governance gap identified in the Architecture Assessment.
- **Negative:** Increased authoring overhead for all future RFCs and ADRs (~30-60 minutes per document for the alternatives section alone).
- **Negative:** Some genuinely simple decisions will feel artificially burdened by the requirement to document alternatives. Authors may push back on the process.
- **Negative:** The qualitative review step adds load to Specification Reviewers, who must now evaluate content quality, not just section presence.
- **Impact on existing contracts:** This ADR modifies the Constitution's "Definition of Approved" quality gate. The Engineering Execution Program §8 now includes a section-header presence check as a pre-condition for Specification Review. Existing RFCs and ADRs are grandfathered and not subject to retroactive amendment.
- **When to revisit:** After 20 documents have been authored under this standard, conduct a retrospective on whether the section requirements are producing genuine value or encouraging padding. Adjust requirements based on evidence, not theory. Additionally, revisit if the CI lint check proves insufficient (e.g., if documents consistently pass CI but fail qualitative review).
